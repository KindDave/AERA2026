Findings (RQ1 & RQ2)
Immediate Behavioral Shifts & Behavioral Diversity  ·  Feedback-Driven Strategic Shifts in IVR Training
RQ 1 How does immediate feedback in an IVR environment influence a learner's behavioral strategies and transitions within a short temporal window (e.g., 30–60 seconds)?
A 30-second pre/post comparison revealed a significant reduction in exploratory behavior (Δ = −0.08, p = .043). At the transition level, self-repetitive instructional loops decreased (IR→IR, p = .009), while transitions from feedback directly to task execution significantly increased (p = .013). Mixed-effects models confirmed the decline in exploratory actions, with more pronounced effects in longer observation windows. Entropy curves consistently peaked between 38–46 seconds after feedback, marking a critical window of heightened behavioral restructuring — the optimal moment for adaptive intervention.

RQ 2 To what extent do observed behavioral shifts and changes in strategic entropy predict improvements in decision quality and overall learning efficiency post-feedback?
While overall task success increased from 11% to 18% post-feedback (not statistically significant), a significant increase in transition entropy was observed (Δ = +0.36, p = .047). Gains in entropy significantly predicted task success (β = 0.23, p = .048), indicating that learning efficiency in IVR is about diversifying strategies, not just speed. Higher decision quality was significantly associated with lower latency and greater behavioral diversity (r = +0.58, p < .001) — confirming that feedback-supported adaptability is the key marker of effective IVR learning. Latency alone was not a significant predictor.
